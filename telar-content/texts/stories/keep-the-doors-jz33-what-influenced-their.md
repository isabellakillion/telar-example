This rhetoric became especially powerful in the 1990s as conservatives increasingly promoted the idea that the United States had entered a “color-blind” era in which racism no longer significantly affected opportunity. If society had already achieved legal equality, opponents argued, then policies that explicitly considered race appeared unnecessary and discriminatory. 

Additionally, evidence shows that support for Proposition 209 was heavily divided along racial and political lines, with white and conservative voters significantly more likely to support the measure. However, scholars note that the opponents of race-based admissions do not acknowledge the ways in which being white has afforded privileges to generations of white people. This view of merit-based fairness ignored the segregation that continued to exist in the 1990s.
